How To Use This Book

This book is most useful when used frequently. The goal is to lower the activation energy required to use rationality techniques. However, odds are that by default, you won't remember to use it when you actually need it. So the first thing you should do is look through The Hourglass Grimoire to decide whether it would be useful, and the second thing you should do is decide whether to embed it in your habits, and implement that decision.

If you do decide you want to use The Hourglass Grimoire in the future, consider creating some Trigger-Action Plans (TAPs). If you don't think TAPs will work for something like this, spend some time planning out some system for using it. Don't forget to use Murphy-Jitsu to check your plan. Continue until you're reasonably confident that you'll use The Hourglass Grimoire when it makes sense to do so.

This book has both digital and print versions. The digital version is a single HTML file; if you want to have it available for use, save the file on your computer or phone. No internet access is required; all resources are embedded in the file.

You can make a print version just by loading the HTML version and using your web browser's Print functionality. (You should use Google Chrome for this; other browsers will not necessarily work.) This will give you all of the instructions and three copies of each worksheet. Double-sided printing is strongly recommended; things are arranged so that worksheets and guidelines on how to use them will be facing pages. After that, you will want to punch holes and put the pages in a three-ring binder. You might also wish to decorate the binder to give it some salience.

Overview

There are many techniques for thinking about things. Some of them, like conversation strategies, are quick things done in the moment. Others are longer, like lists of things to consider. One especially powerful technique is the timer-worksheet. A timer worksheet is a series of questions, each of which has a duration specified; you set a timer on your phone or watch, and focus on that question exclusively until the timer runs out.

Timer worksheets are a major part of the Center For Applied Rationality curriculum. They were originally used as a teaching method with small groups: a timer worksheet keeps everyone on the same page (literally), and gives people quiet time to apply thinking techniques as they're being taught. But it turns out that timer worksheets have significant unanticipated benefits. They free working memory, making multi-step thinking techniques much easier to use. They help with focusing and resisting distractions. Perhaps most important of all, time worksheets lower the activation energy of thinking hard about things that are important.

The Hourglass Grimoire is a collection of timer worksheets, designed to serve as a daily companion and to make the thinking techniques easy to get started on and to follow through to completion. However, this book is not a full explanation of the techniques, nor is it a good way of learning them if they are unfamiliar. While there is explanatory text, these are only reminders; if you start an exercise from its reminder text, you are likely to miss the essence of it.

The Way of the Void

"The primary thing when you take a sword in your hands is your intention to cut the enemy, whatever the means. Whenever you parry, hit, spring, strike or touch the enemy's cutting sword, you must cut the enemy in the same movement. It is essential to attain this. If you think only of hitting, springing, striking or touching the enemy, you will not be able actually to cut him. More than anything, you must be thinking of carrying your movement through to cutting him."
—Miyamoto Musashi, The Book of Five Rings
"Every step of your reasoning must cut through to the correct answer in the same movement. More than anything, you must think of carrying your map through to reflecting the territory. If you fail to achieve a correct answer, it is futile to protest that you acted with propriety."
—Eliezer Yudkowsky, Twelve Virtues of Rationality

This is a book of techniques: specific mental motions meant to achieve effects. It is important not to perform these motions blindly; you must always maintain sight of what it is you're actually trying to achieve, and not to take the specific techniques as more than reminders and suggestions. This is especially true of the timers themselves. Their purpose is to free your working memory from the task of tracking time spent, to help you maintain forward momentum and to resist the urge to flinch away from hard problems.

If a timer would have you stop too soon, set another. If a prompt would have you answer the wrong question, reconsider once, then ignore it. If a timer would have you continue longer than necessary, reconsider twice, then ignore it.

Murphyjitsu

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop, lightly edited.

Murphyjitsu

Murphy's Law says that anything that can go wrong will go wrong, but you can use some of the skills and safeguards you've learned for your Inner Simulator to anticipate and evade these hazards.

Step 1: Pick a plan/project/goal

What's the next action you would need to take to keep this project/plan moving forward? It should be concrete enough that you can picture yourself doing it, not something vague like "work out more."

Step 2: Make it specific enough to visualize

What's the next action you would need to take to keep this project/plan moving forward? It should be concrete enough that you can picture yourself doing it, not something vague like "work out more."

Step 3: Check your surprise-o-meter

Visualize putting this plan in motion, then ask, how surprised would I be if this plan failed? If you'd be shocked, then you're done! Otherwise, continue to step 4.

Step 4: Use Pre-Hindsight

Your plan didn't work! And it failed at the stage of the next action you wrote in Step 2! What happened?

Step 5: Use Looking Forward

What action would you have had to take to prevent this particular failure mode? Visualize taking this preemptive action and then ask "What comes next?" Have you successfully defused the danger? Did you create a new weak point to patch?

Continued on back

Step 3: Check your surprise-o-meter
Step 4: Use Pre-Hindsight
Step 5: Use Looking Forward

Step 6: Iterate!

Repeat steps 3-5 several times (sometimes this technique is called "Simulate 17 times, act once"). What else might have gone wrong? What could have prevented it? You're battle-hardening your plan against happenstance and poor habits. Remember that this should be very quick - all "17" iterations should take maybe a few minutes total.

Making Hard Decisions

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop, with modifications.

Making Hard Decisions

This worksheet is a set of guidelines for making difficult and important decisions. It's pretty in depth and pretty long, so take your time.

Step 1: Perform Sanity Inducing Rituals

Before making a decision, it's important to be in the right frame of mind. To do so, perform some or all of the following:

Againstness
If you're feeling stressed or tense or anything like that, perform the againstness ritual to calm yourself down.
Negative Visualization
When dealing with important decisions, it's easy to get sucked into a frame of mind where you don't even look at all the options, because it makes the decision seem harder and scarier. If you've found it useful in the past, perform negative visualization on the consequences of getting this decision wrong.
Propagate Urges
Propagate urges on actually getting the best answer you can. Don't go into the decision making process with the goal of just making a decision and getting the darn thing out of the way.

Step 2: Value of Information

Before jumping into the decision making process, do a VOI calculation on how important this decision is. Is it worth it to spend several hours on this decision? Or just 15 minutes? Consider the timers for the rest of this worksheet to just be suggestions. If the decision is very important, set up your own timers before moving on.

Step 3: Understand the World

Are there questions you have related to this decision, or areas of uncertainty? Make a list of questions you have, and then try to answer each of them.

Step 4: Understand Your Options

Make a list of all of your available options. Include at least one completely ridiculous option to try to counteract getting anchored on a particular option. Also keep track of the questions you had about the decision. See what different options get generated by different answers to these questions.

Step 5: Steelman Your Options

For each of your options, make the best argument you can for why it's the right choice. Also notice which options you intuitively want to take (keeping in mind that motivation does affect success, but also causes rationalization.)

Step 6: Weigh Your Options

Consider your options again. If your decision is particularly important, do expected value calculations on them. Remember to factor in any uncertainty from the questions you generated earlier. Also do murphyjitsu on each option.

Step 7: End / Iterate

If there is an obvious best choice (or a cluster of choices that are all pretty similar so it doesn't matter) then choose that and execute the decision. If you still have a lot of uncertainty in your models, go back to step 3 and repeat as necessary.

Goal Factoring

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop, with some edits.

Goal Factoring

Goal factoring is a way of systematically reviewing your goals, and your current actions, and making sure the two match up, i.e. making sure the things you do are actually helping you to achieve your goals. In addition, goal factoring is used to come up with new actions that could better achieve your goals.

Step 1: Pick an action

Pick an action that satisfies at least one of the following:

  • You do it frequently.
  • It is costly.
  • You're not sure why you do it.

Step 2: List goals of the action

List the goals you hope to achieve with the action. Remember:

  • There is a difference between wanting to be X and wanting to appear to be X.
  • There is a difference between wanting to do X and wanting to appear to do X.
  • Goals pertaining to interpersonal relationships are still goals!
  • Goals pertaining to social acceptance are still goals!

Step 3: Design better actions

For each of the goals you listed, come up with new ways of achieving that goal. Don’t worry if these new ways are particularly good, just come up with them. Try to focus on actions that can achieve multiple goals at once.

Step 5: Check your plans

Are you unhappy with your new plan? Maybe there’s another goal your old action satisfied that you’ve left out. It’s worth acknowledging your implicit goals, even if you would find them awkward to admit publicly. Add any other goals and come up with ways to achieve them.
Do you expect you will actually follow your new plan? Imagine yourself executing every step of the plan. Notice parts that seem unmotivational or aversive. Notice parts that seem unlikely to work. Revise your plan until you believe you will follow it successfully

Step 6: Implement your new plan(s)

If you developed any new plans, implement them! It's no good if you come up with some great ideas and they just sit on a sheet of paper. If you're not sure how to implement your new plans, try looking at Trigger-Action Planning, Systemization, Offline Habit Training, etc. Also, the five minute timer here is a minimum. Spend at least five minutes on this problem, and then if you aren't finished and want to stop, use whatever reminder system you have in place to make sure you come back to this.

Propagating Urges

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.

Propagating Urges

Step 0: Pick a target

Consider using Propagating Urges whenever you have a task X, for which:

  1. X is useful, is necessary, is something you feel you "have to" do, or otherwise seems to be a pretty good idea to do;
  2. X does not already seem yummy to you; you have no strong urge to complete X.
  3. Nevertheless, you do sometimes do X, e.g. via willpower.
  4. There is no terrible aversion in the mix. E.g., you do not cringe horribly when discussing it.

The urge-gap seems more like "absence of any strong positive motivation" than like "presence of an overwhelming negative motivation". (It’s okay if there are small ughs, along the lines of what most people have about their taxes.)

(If there is a terrible aversion in the mix, do aversion-factoring instead.)

That is (on this view): any time you regularly do a task (e.g., because you "should") and yet you find the task boring, painful, pointless, aversive, or less than fully motivating, this is a state of error. You can fix it by doing propagating urges and/or aversion-factoring. (You might want to build yourself a trigger-response pattern that flags these states, and that suggests you run the procedure then.)

Step 1: Goal-factor

Step 1 of Prop Urges is a particular sort of goal-factoring.

You take the activity that you’d like to find yummy, and put it in an oval on the top of your page. (Example: You might write "Jogging", "Notice mistakes", or "Work on dissertation"). Then, you draw a downward line to a second oval, and, in the second oval, you write just one good thing that that activity accomplishes for you (E.g., below "Notice mistakes", you might write: "Gain power faster").

You win when system 1 and system 2 agree on three things:

  1. You are capable of doing the task. (If your task is "creating a report", your system 1 believes that you can create reports, and that they aren’t just some magic thing that only other people can do.)
  2. The end-goal (the "desirable thing Y" that X will purportedly help with) is desirable and yummy
  3. X really is a good way to achieve Y (there isn’t some other, substantially better/faster/easier, route)

By the time you’ve finished Step 1, the idea of doing a little piece of Step X should sound yummy.

Step 2: Trigger-Affect Planning

Boot up your inner simulator, and imagine going through the task. How would you like to feel as you go through it, if you were the sort of person you want to be? What specific triggers should prompt what affects? If you’re jogging, would you like the burning feeling in your leg muscles to feel good? How about the feeling of the wind in your hair? What kind of "good" would each of these feel?

If there are some aspects of the activity are currently triggering pain, fear, or aversiveness when you do the task, it is often good to re-incorporate those aspects as triggers of some new affect (e.g., to take muscle pain that was feeling aversive, and re-incorporate it as “the feeling of successfully pushing my limits, so I can grow stronger running muscles.”).

The guiding principle here is something like virtue ethics: how would the person you’d most admire feel, as they (went jogging, or noticed a mistake, or did whatever activity you’re considering)?

Step 3: Find a pointer-image, so you can “see it at a glance”. (“Goblin furnace images”)

Again, you’ve finished Step 1: so the task, for the moment at least, sounds yummy. Why aren’t you done? Because it’s temporary: you haven’t yet made the yumminess sticky.

One way to make it sticky would be to revisit all that thinking you did in Step 1, every time you want to write a new paragraph of your dissertation. But that would be slow and cumbersome.

A more efficient thing to do is, after you finish Step 1, to look over it a while until you can “see it at a glance” – much the way you might look over a mathematical proof until you can “see at a glance” why the theorem holds. Then, come up with an image, phrase, song, or other emotional-library item that can serve as a pointer to the analysis you did in step 1: something that can remind you of how & why doing X actually helps toward achieving yummy things that you care about.

Two methods to consider, when crafting images:

  • Pick any movie that is emotionally resonant for you. Link it to your thinking.
  • Look through the list of people you’ve known. Find someone who is memorably good or memorably bad at the thing you’re working on.

Step 4: Fit-check, and mental practice

Now, close your eyes, boot up your inner simulator, and do a “fit check”: imagine going through the situation, this time with your image in mind in the background. (E.g., in Val's example (shared aloud), Val might visualize himself doing pushups, notice how the pain was going to feel, and simultaneously visualize his goblin-furnace image). Imagine the trigger-affect responses playing out as planned. Do you like the fit? Does the new affect pattern suit you? Or does it feel weird, fake, forced, or unlikely to work?

If the new pattern does “sit right” with you, go ahead and practice it several times in your inner simulator, to help it stick. You may also want to try real-life practice, in your “outer simulator”, aka the world.

If the new pattern doesn't sit right with you – if it feels odd, fake, unlikely to work, or forced in some way – revisit step 1. “Stop, drop, and goal-factor”

Appendix 1: Troubleshooting

Ask your "inner simulator" if it expects this process (steps 0-4 above) to work! If the process feels odd or unlikely to work, check these steps:

1. Make sure you actually want your brain to reinforce this action. If you could just press a button and thereby have an urge to do the action, would you press the button? Or might that cause problems? Take a moment to search for good reasons not to acquire this urge, and for any emotional hesitations you might have. Think them through.

2. Make sure your emotional brain agrees with you, about wanting to feel a visceral, in the moment pull toward this action. If you balk emotionally at desiring this new action, your brain will be giving you a little electric shock every time you try to condition yourself toward the habit - and the urge propagation won't work.

3. Make sure you’re using a real causal link to meditate on, experience, and generate images. If you don’t buy (verbally and emotionally) that the goal and the sub-action are really connected, the process might fizzle out.

4. Make sure your emotional brain (and you) are okay with the urge propagation process. If you balk emotionally at the idea of changing your own urges, your brain will (again) be giving you a little electric shock every time you try to link the urge to a new action – and the new feeling won’t stick.

Note that these four trouble-shooting steps are parts of goal-factoring. It is good to do goal-factoring on “be a famous author” and “regularly rewrite paragraphs” before you try to propagate the urge “be a famous author” into the urge “regularly rewrite paragraphs”.

Appendix 2: Urges that are often useful to propagate

A lot of participants (and CFAR staff) have found the following urges particularly useful to propagate. The first set help make common, everyday goals faster and easier. The second help subvert the unconscious, unhelpful conditioning that many people habitually engage in.

We’ve given a few starting points for these urges, to help kickstart your urge propagation process (steps 0-3 above). You’ll need to take some time to find motives that resonate with you.

For everyday goals:
  • Every time you send an email: a fist pumping feeling of “Yes! I just sent an email!”
  • Pomodoros:
    • When you start a pomodoro: “Yes! I’m about to get closer to [vivid thing you like about goal]!”
    • When you finish a pomodoro: “Go me! I just made progress toward [goal]!”
  • When you discard unnecessary things: “Huzzah! I repelled the advancing forces of mess!”
  • When you cut a low-priority item from your todo list: “Woohoo! I just brought into being a better-rested, less-stressed me to do the tasks I really care about!”
For expanding your ability to act/perceive/imagine:
  • When you notice you made a mistake: “Yes! I noticed! Now I have the ability to learn and/or intervene!”
  • When you notice you’re rationalizing: “Wow! I spotted that I’m rationalizing! I can pause and reflect now!”
  • When you notice you’re resisting information/experiencing a mental flinch: “Aha! My brain is trying to hide this from me! I’ll find strength to sally forth into the ugh field!”
  • When you’re going outside your comfort zone: “Ka-ching! I just earned a CoZE point! This is a bit uncomfortable which means I’m exploring!”
  • When you notice that you’re floundering with a new skill or feel like you look dumb/like a novice: “Whew! I’m at the edge of my current competency. This is what real learning feels like.”

Aversion Factoring

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.

Aversion Factoring

Aversion factoring is a method of exploring an aversion you have.

Step 0: Pick an Aversion

Pick an action you are averse to. If you don't already have a list of your aversions, take some time to think of an action that you would like to do, but have difficulty doing.

Step 1: Check your Motivation

First, search for yum-factors. List what consequences of doing X are emotionally appealing / make you go "yum". If there aren't any, consider that you can just drop X.
Goal factor. Are there more efficient ways of achieving the yum factors? Maybe there's a better way to get what you want.
Complementary step: Propagate urges. If X really is a good way to achieve those “yum” factors, does your System 1 know that? When you do X, do you feel emotionally that you are making progress toward your yum factors? If not, you can try to build a stronger emotional connection between them while you do X by propagating urges.

Step 2: List aversion factors

Decide on short “handles” or “filenames” for the various mental and situational factors that make you avoid X or find it bothersome. Some tips:

  • Don't restrict your search to “reasonable” impulses. You might have feelings or other System 1 senses that are more easily summarized or named with phrases like “No one likes me” or “I can't own postage stamps.” Go ahead and write those down as file-names for your feelings, even if you don't reflectively believe them, and give yourself permission to think about them.
  • Include trivial inconveniences as causal factors that make you less likely to do X, e.g. if you don't floss because the floss is kept in a drawer that you don't remember to open.
  • Watch for “Boring” and other red flags: Usually phrases like “it's boring”, “it's hard”, or “takes too much effort” are socially safe ways of saying “I don't like this” without getting into details. But details are key here! Be more specific! E.g. there are many different phenomena that get described as “boredom”:
  • Indignation - I feel like I shouldn't have to do my taxes! Someone else could do it better, and besides, I wish the government wasn't taking my money!
  • Discomfort - Sitting at my desk is physically uncomfortable.
  • Non-engagement - My emotions are pretty dull while I do my taxes; I don't really think much about the pleasantries of not being in jail.
  • Temptations - I wish I went on more dates, and whenever I set aside a big enough chunk of time for taxes, I realize I could use that time to go out and socialize.

Step 3: Start a causal graph

Draw a causal graph of your goal factors, yum factors, aversion factors, and other important aspects of the situation, with arrows depicting the relationships between them. Use thought balloons to distinguish things that are inside your head (like fear of danger) from things that are outside your head (like danger itself).
Before moving on, check for completeness: Imagine the aversion factors were all magically solved... would you then want to go and do X? If not, what's stopping or bothering you about it? Add that factor to the diagram!

Step 4: (For each factor) Assess calibration

For each aversion factor, ask if it is:

  • Worth Solving? Is this aversion alerting me to a real problem that is worth solving?
  • Over-Calibrated? Is my aversion larger or more persistent than the actual problem it's about? Would I push a button to just "get over" this? Are my emotions only protecting me from my emotions here, and can I overcome them with some combination of exposure therapy and big-picture reframing?
  • A Deal Breaker? Is this perhaps a good reason to just avoid X altogether, given my goals?

Annotate your graph with “S” for things that need to be solved, and “C” for aversions that need calibration.
If you find a deal breaker, maybe decide to stop worrying about the aversion for now! If you don't want to forget about it forever, you could try setting a calendar reminder to reconsider it in a few months or years.

Step 5: (For each factor) Solve or calibrate

4a) If there's a problem worth solving, how can you solve it? Get creative! And add short names for the solutions to the graph, with “stopping arrows” toward the problems.
4b) If you want to use exposure therapy and/or reframing, add that to the graph! And jot notes here about the details of the exposure exercise, e.g.. where and when can you do it?

Againstness

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.
  1. Open your body posture. Uncross arms and legs, and create as much space between the bottom of your rib cage and the top of your pelvis as you can.
  2. Take a low, slow, deep breath. Relax as you exhale, and let the exhale go a little longer than the inhale.
  3. Remember your feet. Get into the experience of your feet as presented, and then let your awareness expand to include the rest of your body (but with peripheral body awareness, not with the “laser pointer” of zipping your focus of attention around).
  4. Take another low, slow, deep breath. And enjoy the feeling in your body.

Your Bugs List

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.

Your Bugs List

We refer to anything that systematically goes wrong in your life as a “bug” (in reference to bugs in programming). This can be a glitch in your day-to-day routine, like never being able to find the matching sock. It also includes times when the plan you’re executing is well-designed and going fine, but it isn't getting you what you actually want. Bugs often provide useful information about where your way of doing things can be improved, and thus where you might want to focus your time and energy.

In order to have a chance at fixing your bugs, you need to notice them as they occur. Things to look for include times when:

  • You feel a brief flash of annoyance or frustration
  • You feel conflicted, as if one part of you wants to do one thing and another part of you wants to do something else
  • You need to exert willpower in order to do something
  • Things go differently from how you planned
  • You feel distracted, rather than being engaged in what you are doing
  • It takes you a while to decide what to do
  • Anything else that feels non-ideal, or like something you might be interested in shifting but don’t know how
  • Things are going fine, but somehow you still feel vaguely unsatisfied

Examples of bugs:

  • Forgetting to (or purposely putting off) replying to important emails
  • “Every time I talk to so-and-so I end up in an argument”
  • Only remembering to buy batteries at inconvenient times
  • You submit expense reports on time, but you feel vaguely angsty and bothered whenever you do it.
  • You only start assignments or essays the night before they’re due, even though you hate the stress of last minute all-nighters
  • You find one of your coworkers irritating for reasons that aren’t their fault, but you aren’t able to stop yourself from being frustrated and avoiding collaboration with them.
  • You are generally content with the day-to-day work of your job, but feel dissatisfied with the overall goal of the company you work for
  • You think that you “should” do something, but really don’t want to
  • You’ve been meaning to take a vacation for several years – you even have enough money set aside – but you haven’t gotten around to it.
  • You’re working on your dissertation, and everything is going fine – but when people ask you why you’re doing a PhD, you have a hard time remembering why you wanted to.

Trigger-Action Planning

Trigger-action planning (also called implementation intentions) is a technique for translating goals into actions. It works by picking an action you want to perform, and a situation that will trigger that action.

Some general tips for coming up with good TAPs:

  • Look for TAPs that involve taking advantage of opportunities , dealing with obstacles when they arise, and preventing obstacles from arising to begin with.
  • Look for the weak link in the chain, or the highest leverage change that you can make. What single change to your behavior patterns would produce the largest improvement? For example, if you want to eat more vegetables, think about the step-by-step process that would lead to you eating more vegetables. Would you get more leverage from buying more vegetables at the store, or from having vegetables that are more convenient to eat when you grab a snack, or...?

Step 0: Pick a goal

Pick a goal you would like to achieve or make progress towards.

Step 1: Pick an action

Choose an action that will help achieve your goal. Think about the step-by-step process that would lead to you achieving your goal. Try to locate the point in this process. For example, if your goal is to eat more vegetables, you might A good action is:

Doable
Do not pick an action that you can't picture yourself doing.
Useful
If you do the action, you will make substantial progress towards your goal.

Step 2: Come up with a trigger

Decide what situation you want to trigger this action. A good trigger is:

Visible
The trigger should occur at a specific moment, and you should notice it. So not "before dinner".
Consistent
You should perform the action every time the trigger occurs. So avoid triggers that happen too often.

Gain-pain movies

Gain-pain movies are a technique to increase motivation for acting towards one's long-term goals. With a particular goal in mind:

Step 3.1: Visualize Gain

Fantasize about the wonderful future where you have achieved your goal, focusing on one particularly yummy aspect of that future. Imagine that “gain” in your mind, like you were watching a movie.

Step 3.2: Visualize Pain

Think about the current realities which make it a struggle to advance towards that goal, focusing on one that is particularly difficult or aversive. Imagine that “pain” in your mind, like you were watching a movie.

Step 3.3: Repeat

Repeat steps 1 and 2 two more times, alternating between the two, each time picking a different yummy “gain” in the wonderful future and a different “pain” from the current struggle. Gain-pain movies are especially useful as a lead-in to trigger-action planning.

Step 4: Review

Review your new TAP. Does it seem likely to work? (Use murphyjitsu to test it). If you don't think it's likely to work, either repeat this whole process, or set a reminder to repeat this whole process later if you can't/don't want to right now.

Finding Opportunities for Systemization

What spending attention often feels like

The central goal of everything done in systemization is to free your attention for its more valuable uses. In a moment you're going to do a brainstorm of areas where systemization might help. We'll start by noting common ways that using attention feel so you're more likely to think of them in your own life.

  • Mental effort. You need to remember to do something, or you have to do some mental math. This doesn't apply to things that you're so expert in that they're automatic even though they probably once took your attention and might take others' (e.g. understanding written words).
  • Willpower. You don't want to write that email, but you have to, so you sit down and make yourself do it. This is especially costly if you have to keep choosing to make yourself do it (e.g. you know you could join some friends at a party once you choose to stop working on this email, and the thought repeatedly intrudes and requires another choice to focus).
  • Concentration. When someone is talking nearby or your phone buzzes, you pay in attentional coinage to keep your mind on what you were doing. Using difficult, poorly designed devices or navigating tricky environments also levy a cognitive tax.
  • Decision-making. Do you get dressed before or after breakfast? What should you wear? What are you going to have for lunch? Each decision costs attention, and mulling over a decision costs even more.
  • Task-switching. If you're in the middle of writing something and someone starts a conversation with you, it takes a bit of a mental push to set aside your writing and attend to the conversation. This can be especially costly if there's background to the conversation you have to recall.
  • Multitasking. If you're trying to read email on your phone while listening to a meeting, you're almost certainly paying massive attentional costs, and neither is happening very well relative to your abilities.

A brainstorm of things that might need systems

Mental walkthrough of common routines

Think about things you end up doing often. Where do you notice potential wastes of your attention in...

...your waking-up process (dealing with the alarm, finding glasses, etc.)?

...your getting-dressed process?

...your meals?

...your routines for starting to use a computer (opening a browser & specific websites, etc.)?

...how you begin doing work (whatever you consider “work”)?

...how you connect with friends (emails, Facebook exchanges, hosting events, etc.)?

...how you engage in hobbies?

...anything else?

Mental walkthrough of common dwellings

Now try mentally going through spaces you frequently inhabit or navigate. What is awkward, difficult, annoying, or otherwise attention-sapping in...

...your bedroom?

...your bathroom?

...your kitchen?

...any other space in your home?

...your vehicle (car, bike, or whatever you use)?

...your workspace (e.g. your desk and surrounding area if you have one)?

...your bag (purse, backpack, suitcase, or whatever else like this you use, if any)?

...any other space?

Hamming Prompts

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.

Hamming Prompts

Step 0. Initial thoughts

Did anything come up already? Is anything staring you in the face right now? Now that you have the idea of a "Hamming question" in mind: is anything obvious as your most important problem?

Step 1. Rate-limiting step

The speed of a chemical reaction is determined by the speed of the slowest step - the rate-limiting step. What is yours? Or: what's your bottleneck? Is there a problem where solving it would be the equivalent of "wishing for more wishes?"

Step 2. What are you not allowed to care about?

Or: Is there some good outcome that you generally don't think about because it's too big to picture? Or too impossible?

Step 3. Genre-savviness

When you're reading a novel, sometimes it seems like the book is dragging/stuck because there is an obvious thing that the character needs to do next in order to advance the plot (e.g., clearly she needs to go talk to the magician - can't she just do it already?). If your life is a novel: what is that obvious next thing?

Step 4. What are you already pursuing, badly, in a convoluted/distorted way?

Pica is a medical condition in which people who are iron deficient (for example) eat things like ice cubes, because the signal to eat things with iron is getting distorted into an urge to eat things which share superficial properties with iron. So: what is everything you're doing a pica for?

Step 5. Magnitude of your problems

Which problems in your life have effects that are the largest order of magnitude? You may want to think separately about the effects on you and the effects on the world. For example, for effects on you: If you think about the gap between your current life and a better version of your life, which problem could you solve to cross the largest fraction of that gap? Or, for effects on the world: If you think about the size of your positive impact on the world, which problem could you solve to increase that impact by the largest amount?

Step 6. Focusing

Say aloud: "My life is fine." (Or: "I feel all fine and good about my life.")
If you're like most people, something will catch in your throat. Write it down.
Then imagine putting that thing next to you on the park bench, and go again: "Apart from Project Eggplant, my life is fine in all respects." See what catches now.
For most people, something will catch maybe 3-5 times, and then the sentence will ring true. ("Apart from A, B, C, and D, my life really is fine.")

Step 7. Final go

So, having now gone through all of that: What feels most alive? What is the most important problem, for you right now?

Offline Habit Training

Step 1. Pick a Habit

Choose a habit you want to install. Consider doing a Fermi estimate on the value of practicing it offline for 10 minutes.

Step 2. Consider Alternatives

Are you sure you really want this habit? If you lack motivation, maybe it's time for Goal Factoring and/or Propagating Urges. If you find an action aversive, maybe it's time for Aversion Factoring or CoZE. If there's a skill involved with this habit, e.g. a social or motor skill, maybe it's time for Turbocharging or skill-focused CoZE.

Remember that habits are something you can just try out. So if you're unsure that this habit will be useful, you can just do a test run.

Step 3. Make it Concrete and Repeatable

What's a very concrete and repeatable TAP you could train to help with your new habit? (Concreteness is meant to make it easier for "System 1", particularly procedural memory, to help you with the habit, and repeatability lets you train it many times in a 10-minute time-span.)

Step 5. Design a 5-10 Minute Training Exercise

Come up with an exercise that lets you practice the TAP you developed.

Step 6. Scheduling

Frame-by-Frame Debugging

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.

Frame-by-Frame Debugging

Step 0: Pick a bug

Describe the bug

Pick a recent, concrete example of the bug occurring. Write it down.

Ask Where It Went Wrong

Try to figure out the exact moment at which the bug become unstoppable. This isn't always the obvious moment. If you found you yelled at someone in an argument, then it plausibly went wrong at the start of the argument, not the second before you yelled.

Did You Notice?

In the moment when it all went wrong, did you notice? Some bugs are just problems of forgetting to do anything, and some are problems of not doing anything even though you noticed. (These are not necessarily mutually exclusive).

Get To Noticing

If you didn't notice the bug at the moment it went wrong, complete this step. The goal is to come up with some way to cause yourself to notice the bug in the future. Some useful tools for noticing:

  • TAPs and visualization
  • Offline Habit Training
  • Systemization
  • CoZE

Motivating

If you didn't have enough motivation to stop the bug, complete this step. The goal is to make sure that in the future, if you notice a moment where everything can go wrong, you take a path that stop thats. Some useful tools:

  • Goal factoring
  • Aversion factoring
  • Propagating urges
  • Sanity-inducing rituals

Check the Plan

Regardless of the type of bug, the solution should be a form of generally applicable TAP. If it's possible for this TAP to execute when it shouldn't, you should take some time to revise it so that it doesn't. Next, perform murphyjitsu on the plan.

As a possible final step, try the plan out, expecting it to fail. Pay attention to exactly how everything works, so that you can use the expected failure as a way to get information about improving your plan. (Of course if it doesn't fail you're good.)

Focused Grit

Adapted from the 2015 MIRI Summer Fellows workbook.

Focused Grit

0. Pick a thing you'd like to solve.

You can start by making a list of bugs and then picking one, or if you already have a thing in mind you can skip the list.

Troubleshooting: what thing to pick?

  • Good candidates for things to pick: something you want, something that seems important, something that has been bugging you recently
  • It can be:
    • a personality trait you want to acquire
    • a behavior or emotional pattern you want to shift
    • a factual question you want answered
    • a decision you need to make
  • It can be something specific, something broad/vague, or a specific instance of a broad/vague pattern.

1. Try to solve it... in 5 minutes (yes, actually)

Set a timer for 5 minutes and try to actually solve the problem, so that when the 5 minutes are over the problem is actually solved. Act as if these 5 minutes were the only time that you would ever have to work on the problem (e.g., by using the genie thought experiment described below).

Genie thought experiment. Before you begin step 1 ("Try to solve it... in 5 minutes"), imagine: A genie comes before you and says that after these 5 minutes are up, it will fix your neural patterns about this thing in place so that you will never be able to change it ever again. You have 5 minutes to solve the problem. Go.

2. Brainstorm 5 minute exercises (for 5 minutes)

Set another 5 minute timer and spend those 5 minutes brainstorming ways that you could solve the problem, where each way of solving the problem can itself be done in 5 minutes.

Tips for brainstorming.

  • Write lots of ideas down, even if they seem like terrible ideas which have no chance of working. There is little cost to writing down a bad idea, it's helpful to keep your thoughts flowing, and sometimes good ideas emerge from a pile of terrible ideas.
  • Try imagining that it's someone else's problem and you're giving them advice.
  • Try making predictions instead of decisions ("what would an alternate universe clone of me prefer" not "what should I do?").
  • Notice what the ideas you've generated so far have in common (their shared underlying assumptions), and try to think of ideas which fall outside of that pattern.

3. Do some 5 minute exercises!

Pick one of the more promising 5 minute exercises that you brainstormed in step 2, and do it. After the 5 minutes are up, you can pick a new 5 minute exercise and do that one. If you'd like, you can repeat a third time, and perhaps even a fourth.

Value of Information

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop.

Value of Information

Step 1: Lay Out the Problem

Draw a diagram of the problem. This is often helpful.

Step 2: Consider Currencies

Think about what currencies (time, money, attention, etc.) are most relevant to the situation. What factors are likely to be the most different between the two outcomes? You can start by thinking about this in terms of product features, but ultimately you should cash these out in currencies like time, money, attention, pleasantness of experience, social goodwill, others' happiness, etc).

Step 3: Pick Your Buckets

Decide on how many “buckets” to use in your approximation of the VOI (in this case – and usually - you only need two: “no improvement” vs “plausible improvement”).

Step 4: Estimate Value

Estimate the value of each currency for your “plausible improvement” bucket. This involves estimating the likely size of impact in each of your currencies. Remember to streamline your process whenever possible by ignoring currencies that are much less variable or important than the most important factor. (Tip: when trying to translate between currencies, use thought experiments! This can work well for readily quantifiable currencies (such as time and money), or currencies traditionally considered less quantifiable (such as my mother's happiness, or experiencing beauty). Try to isolate one variable for each experiment).

Step 5: Convert Currencies

Convert all your currencies to a single currency. Time and money are often the easiest choices.

Add Everything Up

Add all of your different currencies up.

Making Large Updates

By James Babcock.

Making Large Updates

1. Clarify.

How, exactly, have your beliefs changed? Write down what you used to believe, what you believe now, and how these differ, as clearly and succinctly as you can.

2. Check.

Are you confident that this change is correct? Take a few minutes to honestly evaluate the strength of the evidence.

If yes, continue.
If no, do not continue with this sheet. Instead, you may which to use Focused Grit or another technique to determine whether the update is correct.

3. Trace the change.

Was there a specific observation that inspired the change? Was this the result of thinking seriously about something for the first time?

4. Trace the old belief.

How long did you hold the old belief, and what caused you to do so? Does this suggest a reference class in which more false beliefs might be found?

5. Write implications of the old belief.

You will want to reevaluate these later, but you should avoid rushing too quickly to the conclusion that they're untrue.

6. Write implications of the new belief.

What are some consequences your now beliefs have? Given what you know now, are any longstanding mysteries now ready to resolve?

7. Check for confusion.

Do you notice anything which still seems to not make sense? Are there new questions that now require thought?

Crystallizing Concepts

By James Babcock.

Crystallizing Concepts

Sometimes, when thinking about a topic you may notice concepts - distinctions, patterns or clusters in thing-space - which seem like they might be broadly useful. It's useful to take those concepts, and crystallize them: clarify your thinking, and store the concept in your brain in a way that will make you use it later.

1. Clarify the Concept

What is the distinction, pattern, or cluster that you are gesturing towards? How would you tell whether it applied to something or not? Take a few minutes to clarify your thinking.

2. Come up with examples

Try to come up with three different examples where this concept seems to apply. If this is difficult, consider going back to (1) and re-defining the concept as something less specific, or discarding the concept entirely.

3. Check for pre-existing work

Does this concept already have a name? Try to think of one-word descriptions which try to capture it, and phrases which others might have used to describe this concept. Try typing a few of them into a search engine.

4. Give the concept a name

A good name for a concept is transparent: it should give people some idea of what you're talking about without further explanation. If the name must be based on a reference or an analogy, avoid references and analogies that would seem like in-group signals. Good concept-names are also short, and able to be adapted into several different parts of speech ("stemmed").

Turbocharging Suggestions

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop with modifications.

Turbocharging Suggestions

Turbocharging is a way to increase the rate at which you learn a skill. The goal is to develop a practice method for whatever skill you're trying to acquire.

Practice the Skill You Want

It's very easy to get caught up practicing something that "feels associated" with the skill you actually want. Don't do this! Make sure whatever practice you do is direct practice of the skill you want, or as close to that as possible. If you want to learn how to have conversation in another language, your practice must include actually having conversations in that language.

Scale Difficulty

Don't get caught in the trap of starting out with something hard, and then continuing to do that thing after it stop being hard. If you want to acquire a skill, you should always be working on the edge of your capability. If you feel like you aren't improving, you might need to calibrate the difficulty of your current practice.

Don't Burn Out

Because turbocharging requires you to constantly be pushing yourself, it is very tiring. Watch out for practice becoming aversive. Also, if you're feeling demotivated because you're constantly doing difficult things so it never feels like it's getting easier, try going back to one of your old practice routines and doing that, just for fun.

Systemization Suggestions

Excerpted from the CFAR April 2015 workshop with modifications.

Systemization Suggestions

Systemization is a technique for freeing up attention, by developing a system for handling a particular task. This is especially useful for repeated tasks (grocery shopping, paying bills, taxes, etc.)

Goals of Systemization

When creating a system, you should be trying to achieve several things:

Effortlessness
Ideally your system should work perfectly once you set it up, without additional input from you. Try to avoid systems that require ongoing discipline or continually making decisions.
Reliability
Make sure the reminders in your system are reliable. If you ask a person to remind, there's a pretty good chance they'll forget eventually. If your reminders are reliable, you will be able to relax and trust your system to let you know when to take action.
Invisiblity
Try to make your reminders invisible when they're not needed. If you're constantly seeing the reminders at times when you don't need reminding, you might start ignoring them. For instance, if you make a checklist for washing the dishes, try to put the checklist somewhere you will only see when you're about to wash the dishes.

Some Types of System

Set External Reminders
Create some system of reminders. Alarms, calendar notifications, Boomerang, or any other preferred system. Calendar apps are pretty good for this, but there are plenty of options.
Establish a Routine
If the action can be turned into a routine, do so. Then practice the routine a few times.
Create a Checklist
If the action consists of a sequence of steps, or you find you're forgetting parts of it, create a checklist. For instance, if you find you frequently forget stuff at home, create a checklist for leaving the house (apartment/domicile/etc.)
Outsource
Get someone else to do it. If it's important enough, consider paying someone else to do it.

Appendix: How to Extend the Hourglass Grimoire

The Hourglass Grimoire is an HTML file, and as such, it can be edited in a normal text editor. The canonical copy lives in a private git repository on GitHub, https://github.com/jimrandomh/TheHourglassGrimoire. You can email jimrandomh@gmail.com for access, but you only need this to coordinate development with other writers; to make your own customizations, you can just edit the file.

The Hourglass Grimoire differs from the sorts of HTML you may be used to in a few important ways. First, it is meant to be carried around as a single file and used offline. This means that all images, scripts, stylesheets, sounds and other resources must be embedded in the file directly, not referred to by URL. For Javascript and CSS, this is just a matter of putting anything that might otherwise be a separate file inline in a <script> or <style> tag. For images and sound effects, you can inline them using a data: URL. There is a convenient file-to-URL converter at http://dataurl.net/#dataurlmaker. While there is no technical size limit for this (size limits mentioned on dataurl.net refer to obsolete browser versions), we'd prefer to keep the total file size of the Grimoire to less than a megabyte unless there's a good reason not to.

The presence of binary blocks and general non-splitting of this into multiple files can make it awkward to edit. If your text editor supports marker-based folding, you can use this feature to make it more manageable by folding up (hiding) the portions you aren't working on. The convention is that {{{ marks the start of a foldable region, and }}} marks the end of a foldable region. In vim, you can enable folding by typing this after opening the file:

:set foldmethod=marker
:set foldmarker={{{,}}}

Even if you aren't using folding, it would be helpful to still include the fold markers when you add sections.

Print Issues

The second major difference from normal HTML is that The Hourglass Grimoire is designed for usage both in a web browser, and also in print. Unfortunately, web-browser support for pagination and page numbering is fairly bad, and the workarounds used have a few side effects. The main issue is that the page numbering is not quite automatic; while the page numbers in the table of contents and other cross-references are filled in automatically, the placement of page-breaks is entirely manual. The division between pages is done by wrapping blocks of content in

<div class="numberedPage">
    One page's worth of content
</div>

It's important that all content (which is to be included in the print version) is wrapped this way, and that one numberedPage element does not overflow to more than a page's worth of space. Content which isn't wrapped will appear on pages with no page numbers on them. If one numberedPage element exceeds one page's worth of content, only the first page will have a page number on it.

Creating cross-references with page numbers is fairly easy; it's the same as a normal in-page anchor link, with the page number filled in automatically; the only difference is that you add the pageNumberRef class to the anchor, eg:

<li>
    <a href="#TableOfContents" class="pageNumberRef">Table of Contents</a>
</li>

Looks like:

  • Table of Contents

    • In general, if you are making edits, you should make sure to look at both the in-browser and print versions. (You don't need to actually print; Chrome's Print Preview is accurate enough for this purpose.

    Left and Right Pages

    When printed with two-sided printing and placed in a binder, odd-numbered pages are "right" pages while even-numbered pages are "left" pages. (These numbers refer to the raw numbering sequence, not the one that is displayed in the corner of the page, since that sequence skips some pages).

    By convention, the title page of each section is a right page, and the first page of each worksheet is also a right page. This means that the last page of instructions faces the first page of the worksheet. If the instructions are more than one page long, then this should be some sort of summary page. Note that this means that instructions are always an odd number of pages, while worksheets are always an even number of pages. Insert blank pages if necessary to make this true.

    Worksheet Extraction and Layout

    Sections are written with instructions and worksheets mixed together. This is rendered three different ways: Instructions, Worksheet, and Mixed, containing different subsets of the elements. When you use the Grimoire in your browser, this is Mixed view. When you print, you will by default get one copy of the Instructions view, followed by three copies of the Worksheet view. The idea is that the Instructions view contains all the long text, while the Worksheet view is mostly empty space to write in, but they have the same sequence of prompts. The Instructions view pages are numbered, while the Worksheet view pages aren't (since there are meant to be multiple copies, and copies are meant to be inserted and removed).

    Worksheets are manually paginated, but not with the "numberedPage" elements (which determines page-breaks for the instructions view and miscellaneous content). Instead, worksheet page-breaks are created with a .worksheetPageBreak element:

    <div class="worksheetPageBreak></div>

    The blank spaces for questions on worksheets are automatically sized. Each prompt that should have a blank space under it should have a "weight" attribute, which is a number; the sizes of the blank spaces are proportional to weight; eg if a page contains an element with weight="2" and an element with weight="1", then two-thirds of the space will go to the first element.
